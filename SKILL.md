# How to Find What You Want to Do
*Based on the Self-Awareness Method by Jinpei Yagi*

## Core Idea

People are lost not because they lack passion, but because they don't know themselves. The **Self-Awareness Method** is a systematic framework — not intuition — that helps you discover:

> **What You Truly Want to Do = Values (Why) × Strengths (How) × Passion (What)**

These three pillars must overlap. Finding this intersection is the work.

---

## How to Use This Skill

This is a **coaching framework, not a lecture.** When someone is feeling lost or asks how to find their direction, do NOT dump all three pillars and the whole method at once — that overwhelms people and helps no one. Run it as a guided conversation instead.

**1. Diagnose where they already are.** Before teaching anything, find out what they've already figured out. Do they have any sense of their values? Do they know what they're good at? Meet them at their actual starting point rather than starting from zero.

**2. Work one pillar at a time, in method order.** The order matters (see "The Three Rules"): **Values → Work Purpose → Strengths → Passion.** Don't move to the next pillar until the current one has something concrete in it. Briefly explain the pillar you're on, then move to questions.

**3. Ask, then wait.** Pull just 2–3 questions from the relevant bank in `references/questions-en.md`, ask them, and *stop* to let the person answer. Build the next step on what they actually said. This is a back-and-forth over multiple turns, not a worksheet you hand over.

**4. Bring in the reference files at the right moment.** Surface the lists only when they help — e.g. show the values list (`references/values-en.md`) when someone is stuck naming keywords, or the strengths list when they can't see their own talents. Don't paste a 100-item list unprompted.

**5. Synthesize at the end.** Once they have material in each pillar, help them combine Passion × Strengths into candidate "things I want to do," then filter through their Work Purpose (see "Finding Your True Calling"). 

Keep the tone warm and curious. One focused question at a time lands better than five. The goal is *their* self-discovery — you're the guide, not the answer key.

## Why People Stay Lost

1. **Too many options** — Columbia University's "Jam Study": 24 jam options → 3% buy; 6 options → 30% buy. More choice causes paralysis.
2. **Wrong question** — "What's most advantageous?" keeps you lost. The right question is: "What do I *want* to do?"
3. **Misconceptions** that block the search:

| Misconception | Truth |
|---|---|
| Must be a lifelong commitment | Do "what you most want to do *now*" |
| You'll feel destiny when you find it | It starts as mere interest |
| Must benefit others | Living for yourself also helps others |
| Need to act first | Know yourself first |
| Can't become a job | The "how" lives in society; the "what" lives in you |
| Follow your gut | Use a systematic method |

## The Three Pillars

### Pillar 1: Values (重要的事) — WHY
> *Your life direction. The compass, not the destination.*

**Values = Life Purpose + Work Purpose**
- *Life Purpose* (internal) — how you want to live, for your own sake (e.g. "I want to love life")
- *Work Purpose* (external) — the impact you want to have on others (e.g. "help more people love life")

Values describe **how you want to live (Being)**, not **what you want to do (Doing)**. Get the life purpose right first — satisfy yourself by living it, and a work purpose for others follows (see the overflow principle below).

**Values vs. Goals**
- Values = the direction you keep walking
- Goals = checkpoints along the way
- Setting goals without values = blind ambition. Even when achieved, you feel hollow.

**True vs. False Values**
- True values: "I want to live this way!" — no right answer, no need for others' approval
- False values: "I should live this way" — inherited from parents, society, others

**How to find your values (5 steps):**

1. **Answer 5 questions** — extract value keywords (minimum 15 keywords)
   - *If you get stuck:* writing meditation (write for 3 min without stopping) or ask a friend to interview you
2. **Build a mind map** — group similar keywords together
3. **Shift from other-centered to self-centered values** — avoid values you can't control (e.g. "be respected by others"). Ask: *"What is my real purpose? What would I do once that's achieved?"*
4. **Rank your values** — ask: "Which is my ultimate purpose?" Reduces future confusion
5. **Define your Work Purpose** — your work purpose = spreading your values to others (after first fulfilling them in yourself). Review 10 experiences where you provided value to others; the most recurring keyword is your work purpose

> **Warning:** "Making people happy" or "making people smile" is NOT a work purpose — it's too vague. Every job can do that. Dig deeper.

**Fill your own cup first (the overflow principle).** Work purpose isn't something you force outward through willpower — it forms naturally, in a specific order. First you live by your own values until *you* feel satisfied: if one of your values is "security," you do the things each day that make *you* feel secure. Picture your values as water filling a cup. Once the cup is full, the water overflows on its own and spreads to the people around you — and *that* overflow is your work purpose. You can't pour from an empty cup, so trying to deliver value to others before you've satisfied yourself leaves you drained and resentful. Satisfy yourself first; the outward mission follows.

**Check each value by asking:** "Do I *want* this, or do I *feel I should* have this?" Any "should" or "have to" is a false value.

> **Still stuck after steps 1–5?** Go to the work-purpose questions (marked `***`) in `references/questions-en.md` — they're designed to pull a work purpose out when the five core questions don't.

### Pillar 2: Strengths (擅长的事) — HOW
> *Your natural talents — unconscious habits that produce results effortlessly.*

**Definition:** Things you do better than others naturally, without strain, that feel smooth and energizing.

Another way to put it: a strength is an **unconscious habit** — a pattern of thinking, feeling, or acting you run without noticing. Habits aren't good or bad in themselves; whether one is an asset or a liability depends on how you use it. Concrete examples of the kind of habit that signals a strength:
- always observing the people around you
- always acting the moment an idea strikes
- always reading how others are feeling
- always thinking about how to win
- always thinking about how to make others happy

**Strengths vs. Skills/Knowledge**
| Strengths (Talents) | Skills & Knowledge |
|---|---|
| Natural, unconscious | Learned over time |
| Applicable to any work | Only applies to specific work |
| Makes you increasingly free | Can trap and limit you |

**Signs you've found a strength:**
- Enjoyable while doing it
- Done unconsciously and well, without effort
- No pressure → easy to get absorbed
- Feels like "being yourself"
- You do it naturally even outside work
- You wonder: "Why can't others do this?"

**Turning weaknesses into strengths:**
Replace "I'm introverted, *so* I can't make friends easily" → "*Because* I'm introverted, I build deep, meaningful relationships with the people who matter."

The key insight: A strength and a weakness are often the same trait viewed differently. Like the "young woman / old woman" optical illusion — it depends on your angle.

**Why build on strengths (not fix weaknesses):** Working on what you're bad at is like living with your non-dominant hand. No matter how hard you train, someone who switches between hands can never beat someone using their dominant hand the whole time — and a fish will never win by practicing flying. So the first question isn't "how do I fix my flaws?" but *"What am I actually built for — am I a fish that swims, or a bird that flies?"*

**Speed-reading study:** Group A read 90 words/min → after 3 years training → 150/min. Group B read 350 words/min → after 3 years training → 2,900/min. Developing existing strengths yields exponentially more than fixing weaknesses.

**The "Halo Effect":** If you have one outstanding strength, people overlook your weaknesses and see you as exceptional overall. Sharpen your star points — don't sand them down to become a bland circle.

**5 Questions to Find Your Strengths:**
1. What experiences in your life felt most fulfilling? *(Doing strengths = energizing, not draining)*
2. What has frustrated or irritated you recently? *(Irritation reveals a strength you take for granted — you're annoyed because something that's effortless for you is something others can't do)*
3. Ask people close to you: "What do you think my strengths are?"
4. If you quit your job tomorrow, what part of it would you miss most?
5. What successes have you had, and *how* did you achieve them? *(The most important question — analyze from 8 angles)*

After answering, compile your strengths into a **Personal User Manual** — rate each with:
- ★★★ Fulfilling AND linked to past success
- ★★ Fulfilling
- ★ Unclear yet

### Pillar 3: Passion (喜欢的事) — WHAT
> *The domain that ignites your curiosity — not a job title, but a field.*

**Definition:** An area that makes you naturally ask "Why? How? What if?" — the field you want to understand more deeply.

**Examples:**
- Loves self-awareness → "How can I understand myself better?"
- Loves programming → "Why won't this system run?"
- Loves ramen → "What makes one bowl better than another?"

**Passion beats effort in the long run:**
- Short race: effort wins
- Marathon (= life): passion beats effort
- "Working for money" cannot compete with "working for love of the subject"

As Da Vinci put it: eating without appetite harms the body, and in the same way, studying without the desire to learn damages the memory. Find a field that makes you involuntarily think *"I want to know more!"* and you'll never have to manufacture motivation.

**Critical mistake:** "I love baseball, so I'll work in baseball" is wrong. The right approach: *What aspect of baseball do I love?*
- Love teamwork → work involving collaboration
- Love mastering skills through dedication → craft-oriented work
- Love strategy → analytical work

**"Interest-based" passion (can be work) vs. "Utility-based" passion (cannot):**
- ✓ "I do this because I love it in the moment" → can become meaningful work
- ✗ "I do this because it's useful/profitable" → will drain you over time

Traps that kill passion discovery: only doing what's useful, only doing what impresses others, only doing what earns money, only doing what produces results.

**5 Questions to Find Your Passion:**
1. What would you spend money to learn or experience right now?
2. What types of books are on your shelves? *(Notice what you're drawn to intuitively, not what seems "useful")*
3. What has saved you, made you think "This changed my life!"?
4. Whose work do you want to thank — whose work touched you deeply?
5. What makes you angry about the current state of the world? *(Anger = you see a gap between what is and what could be)*

## The Three Rules (Order Matters)

1. **Rule 1: Start with Values, not Passion**
   Passion is the *means*; values are the *purpose*. "Work I love" satisfies me, but it doesn't automatically provide value to others. Value to others = income. So: find your Work Purpose from your values *first*.

2. **Rule 2: Find Strengths before Passion**
   Many people can't find their passion because they secretly believe "even if I found it, I couldn't make it work." Knowing your strengths gives you the confidence that you *can* make anything work.

3. **Rule 3: Don't think about implementation yet**
   Finding "what I truly want to do" before deciding *how* to do it. Deciding on "blogging, YouTube, coding, job-hopping" before knowing your purpose is like choosing a plane or car before deciding your destination.

**Summary sequence:**
Values → Work Purpose → Strengths → Passion → *What You Truly Want to Do* → Implementation

## Finding Your "True Calling" (Two Steps)

### Step 1: Passion × Strengths = Candidate List
- List your passions and strengths separately
- Mark strengths with ★★★ that naturally match your passions
- Combine them freely — quantity over quality at this stage

### Step 2: Filter through Work Purpose
From your candidates, ask: *"Which of these lets me deliver my work purpose to others?"*

Money = "Thank you" from people you've helped. You are not paid for what you *want* to do; you are paid when what you want to do *also provides value to others*.

### Iterating the "How" — Bend the Method to Your Strengths

Finding your calling isn't one-and-done. Passion (what) and strengths (how) rarely line up perfectly on the first try, so you keep reshaping the *method* until the work runs through your strengths. Doing something you love but are *bad at* still feels flat — fix the how, not the what.

The author's own example: he loves self-awareness (passion) but is weak at *listening* and strong at *speaking and writing*. So he kept bending the method:
- 1-on-1 coaching (lots of listening) → drained him
- → Seminars: he speaks to 50 people at once (plays to speaking)
- → Recorded video courses: he records once, then answers follow-up questions by message (avoids repetition, which he also dislikes)

Same passion throughout; the *how* evolved to fit who he is.

**When a way of working feels off, check it against 3 angles:**
- Is it drifting from my **values**?
- Is it drifting from my **strengths**?
- Is it drifting from my **passion**?

(Note: this judges your *working method*. It's different from the job-choosing framework further down, which is about which industry/role/company to pick.)

## The "Color Bath" Effect

Once you've found your true calling, **implementation is simply closing the gap between the version of you who has achieved it and the you of today.** And that gets easier than expected, because of the color bath effect: once you know what you want to do, your brain starts noticing relevant opportunities everywhere — like being told to spot red objects. The world doesn't change; your attention does. This is why clarity of purpose accelerates real-world progress.

---

## Key Mindsets

- Stop living "for the future." Living fully for what you want *now* creates more growth than preparing for a vague future.
- "What I want to do" starts as a hypothesis. Use the loop: **Hypothesize → Act → Reflect → Apply**. Blind action without self-knowledge is gambling — like hoping to win the lottery.
- Failure and regret, seen through self-awareness, become experience. Nothing is wasted.
- Success = "living as yourself, this very moment." Great success = living as yourself *and* producing results.

## Quick Reference: Question Banks

The **full banks (30 questions each)** are in `references/questions-en.md`. A few starters for each:

### For Values
- Who impresses you most, and which of their traits?
- At 80, what would you wish you'd spent more time on?
- What's the most unforgivable thing you've experienced? *(→ reveals deep values)*
- *(Work purpose)* What impact do you want to bring the people around you?

### For Strengths
- What have you been naturally good at since childhood?
- What do you catch yourself thinking "how can people *not* do this?" about?
- What could you do for hours without tiring?
- What did parents or teachers keep reminding you about? *(often where you stand out)*

### For Passion
- Setting money and practicality aside, what do you love?
- What books do you buy without needing a "reason"?
- What thing or person gave you the feeling "this saved me!"?
- What in society makes you angry enough to want to fix it?

## Career Decisions Framework

When evaluating a job change or new role:
- **Passion** = Why did you choose this industry?
- **Strengths** = How will you produce results in this role?
- **Values** = Why this company specifically?

Work is sustainable only when *both* sides hold — you don't tire of it AND the customer needs it. This gives three outcomes:

| | You don't tire of it | Customer needs it | Sustainable? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Good work** | ✓ | ✓ | Yes |
| **Hobby** | ✓ | ✗ | No — no one pays for it |
| **Tiresome work** | ✗ | ✓ | No — you burn out |

- "You don't tire of it" comes from **Strengths + Passion**
- "Customer needs it" comes from **Values alignment** → genuine value delivery
